# Geography and the monsoon

The South Asian subcontinent is bounded by the Himalayas to the north and the
Indian Ocean to the south. Three great river systems shape the region: the
Indus in the west, the Ganges across the northern plains, and the Brahmaputra
in the east. The fertile Indo-Gangetic plain has supported dense agricultural
populations for thousands of years.

The defining climatic feature is the monsoon. From June to September the
southwest monsoon brings heavy rains that the entire agricultural cycle depends
on. A weak monsoon historically meant failed harvests and famine, so its timing
shaped settlement, trade, and politics across the subcontinent.

Because the Himalayas block cold air from the north, the northern plains stay
warmer than their latitude would suggest, which lengthens the growing season.
